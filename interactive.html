The Main Result

One equation connects fluid dynamics, prime numbers, and the approach to randomness

Every prime has a gap to its neighbor: 3−2=1, 5−3=2, 7−5=2, 11−7=4, ... Mathematicians have long believed these gaps approach a Poisson process (like radioactive decay) as primes get large. But nobody has found the equation that drives this convergence.

We define a velocity field from the prime gap distribution:

Definition
u(v) = − d/dv [log f(v)]
where f(v) is the probability density of normalized gaps v = g/log(x)

When gaps follow the Poisson distribution (f = e−v), this velocity is u = 1 everywhere — uniform flow. The sieve of Eratosthenes creates deviations from this uniform flow.

We prove that this velocity field satisfies:

The Fisher-KPP term u(1−u) emerges naturally from the mathematics — it is not imposed. It pushes u toward 1 from either side: if u > 1 (some gaps too likely), it corrects down; if u < 1, it corrects up. u = 1 is the unique stable equilibrium = Poisson.

Where Does the Equation Come From?

Three steps: a stochastic process, its Fokker-Planck equation, and a change of variables

Step 1: The Feller Process

Consider the stochastic differential equation:
dV = (1 − V) dt + √(2V) dW

This is a Feller diffusion: mean-reverting toward V=1 with noise proportional to √V. Small gaps fluctuate little (tightly constrained by the sieve); large gaps fluctuate freely. Its stationary distribution is Exp(1) = e−v.

Step 2: The Fokker-Planck Equation

The probability density f(v,t) of the Feller process satisfies:
ft = v·f'' + (v+1)·f' + f

This operator has Laguerre polynomial eigenfunctions e−vLn(v) with eigenvalues λn = −n. Spectral gap = 1.

Step 3: Change to Velocity

Define u = −(log f)'. Then differentiate the Fokker-Planck equation. After exact algebraic manipulation (every step verified symbolically), u satisfies the Burgers-Fisher equation.

The Burgers nonlinearity 2vu·uv appears from the chain rule applied to the nonlinear transformation u = −f'/f. It is the same quadratic nonlinearity that appears in the Navier-Stokes equations.

The Spectral Gap: Why Poisson Wins

The equation's spectrum guarantees that perturbations die out

Linearizing around u = 1, the perturbation δu = u − 1 satisfies:

∂(δu)/∂t = v·(δu)'' + (2−v)·(δu)' − δu

This operator's eigenfunctions are the associated Laguerre polynomials Ln(1)(v) with eigenvalues λn = −(n+1):

Spectral gap = 1: every perturbation mode decays exponentially. If the sieve forcing also decays (as O(1/(log x)β)), the velocity field converges to u = 1. This is the mechanism by which prime gaps approach Poisson.

Watching the Velocity Field Converge

The sieve creates ripples in the velocity; they flatten as primes get larger

At small scales (103), the velocity field oscillates around u=1. At large scales (108), the oscillations diminish. The flow is smoothing out toward uniform — toward Poisson.

The Sieve Diffusion Model

Starting from pure randomness (Cramér model, no sieve), we add sieve primes one by one. Adding p=3 alone captures 74% of the deviation from the empirical distribution. Beyond p=7, additional primes barely matter.

Open Problems

What remains to be proven

1. Derive the sieve forcing decay

Show that the sieve perturbation ||φ|| = O(1/(log x)β) follows from the Hardy-Littlewood conjecture and equidistribution of primes in arithmetic progressions.

2. Joint convergence

Our theorem governs the marginal gap distribution. Proving MI → 0 requires extending the framework to the joint distribution of consecutive gaps.

3. Compute the exponent

The observed α ≈ 0.19 should be derivable from the pore strength δ3 = 1 and the equidistribution rate of gaps mod 3. This would give a first-principles convergence rate for the Gallagher conjecture.
